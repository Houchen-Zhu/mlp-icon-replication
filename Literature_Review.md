# Literature Review and Small-Scale Replication: ICON-OCnet for Optimal Execution

## 1. Introduction

Optimal order execution is a central problem in quantitative finance. When an institutional trader needs to buy or sell a large position, executing the entire order immediately may create substantial price impact and lead to unfavorable execution prices. A standard solution is to split a large meta-order into smaller child orders and execute them over a finite time horizon. The trader must balance several forces: trading too quickly increases instantaneous transaction costs and price impact, while trading too slowly leaves inventory exposed to market risk and may fail to complete the liquidation objective.

The paper *Solving Optimal Execution Problems via In-Context Operator Networks* proposes a framework called ICON-OCnet for solving optimal execution problems when the price impact mechanism is unknown. The central idea is to avoid assuming a fixed parametric price impact model. Instead, the paper uses in-context operator learning to infer the current price impact operator from a few execution examples, and then uses this inferred operator inside a neural-network-based optimal control solver.

The methodology consists of two main components:

1. ICON: an in-context operator network that learns the mapping from trading-rate trajectories to price-impact trajectories.
2. OCnet: a neural network policy that solves the downstream optimal execution problem using the ICON-inferred surrogate price impact operator.

This review summarizes the main modeling setup, explains the ICON-OCnet methodology, discusses its contribution relative to traditional optimal execution approaches, reports a small-scale replication of the ICON learning step, and outlines potential future research directions.

## 2. Background: Optimal Execution and Price Impact

The classical optimal execution problem considers a trader who begins with an initial inventory $x$ of a risky asset and wishes to liquidate the position over a finite time horizon $[0,T]$. The trader chooses a selling rate process $u_t$, and the remaining inventory evolves as

$$
X_t
=
x-\int_0^t u_s\,ds .
$$

Here:

- $u_t$ is the instantaneous selling rate at time $t$;
- $X_t$ is the remaining inventory at time $t$;
- $x$ is the initial inventory.

In discrete time, if the interval is divided into $N$ steps with step size $\Delta t$, then the amount sold during one time step is approximately

$$
\Delta q_i
=
u_{t_i}\Delta t ,
$$

and the inventory is updated by

$$
X_{t_i}
=
x-\sum_{j=0}^{i-1}u_{t_j}\Delta t .
$$

The trader's execution price is modeled as

$$
P_t
=
S_t-Y_t ,
$$

where $S_t$ is the unaffected price process, representing the price that would have prevailed without the trader's own execution, and $Y_t$ is the price impact generated by the trader's trading activity.

The price impact is assumed to depend on the current and past trading rates:

$$
Y_t
=
\bigl(I_\theta(u)\bigr)_t .
$$

The operator

$$
I_\theta:
L^2([0,T])
\longrightarrow
L^2([0,T])
$$

maps an entire trading-rate trajectory $u$ to an entire price-impact trajectory $Y$. This is why the paper describes the problem as an operator learning problem rather than a standard function approximation problem. The input is not a single scalar; it is a path. The output is also a path.

The trader's objective is to maximize a performance functional that incorporates execution revenue, price impact costs, instantaneous trading costs, inventory penalties, and terminal liquidation penalties. In one common representation, the objective can be written as

$$
J(u)
=
\mathbb{E}
\left[
\int_0^T
\left(
  \alpha_t-\bigl(I_\theta(u)\bigr)_t
\right)
u_t\,dt
-
\varepsilon\int_0^T u_t^2\,dt
-
\phi\int_0^T X_t^2\,dt
-
\varrho X_T^2
\right]
+
x\mathbb{E}[S_T] .
$$

Here:

- $\alpha_t$ is the intraday alpha signal;
- $\varepsilon$ penalizes instantaneous trading speed;
- $\phi$ penalizes running inventory;
- $\varrho$ penalizes terminal inventory;
- $I_\theta$ is the price impact operator;
- $\bigl(I_\theta(u)\bigr)_t$ is the price impact at time $t$ generated by the trading path $u$.

The goal is

$$
J(u)
\longrightarrow
\max_u .
$$

In the paper's numerical experiments, the authors focus on the deterministic base case where the unaffected price process is a martingale, so that

$$
\alpha_t \equiv 0 .
$$

In that case, the optimal strategy depends mainly on the price impact operator, the cost parameters, and the initial inventory.

## 3. The Main Challenge: Unknown Price Impact

Traditional optimal execution models usually begin by specifying a particular price impact model. For example, one might assume an exponential decay kernel, a power-law decay kernel, or some other parametric structure. After choosing the model class, one estimates the corresponding parameters from data and solves the optimal control problem under that model.

The paper argues that this parametric approach is restrictive in real trading. Price impact is difficult to estimate reliably because market conditions are non-stationary, liquidity regimes change over time, and the true relationship between trading activity and price response may not be captured by a simple parametric model.

The main contribution of ICON-OCnet is to replace explicit parameter estimation with in-context operator learning. Instead of estimating parameters such as $\lambda$, $\beta$, or $\gamma$, ICON attempts to infer the relevant price impact operator directly from a few execution examples.

This is the key methodological shift:

$$
\text{traditional approach: data}
\longrightarrow
\widehat{\theta}
\longrightarrow
I_{\widehat{\theta}}
$$

versus

$$
\text{ICON approach: context examples}
\longrightarrow
\widehat{I}_{\theta}.
$$

ICON does not need to output explicit model parameters. It only needs to provide a usable surrogate operator that maps candidate trading paths to predicted price impact paths.

---

## 4. ICON: In-Context Price Impact Operator Learning

ICON is designed to learn the mapping

$$
u
\longmapsto
Y
$$

in an in-context manner. The important point is that ICON is not trained as an ordinary supervised learning model with only $u$ as input and $Y$ as output. Instead, its input consists of already observed execution-impact examples plus a new query execution path.

A typical ICON task has the form

$$
\left[
  (u^1,Y^1),
  \ldots,
  (u^M,Y^M),
  u^0
\right]
\longmapsto
Y^0 .
$$

Here:

- $(u^1,Y^1),\ldots,(u^M,Y^M)$ are context examples from the same price impact environment;
- $u^0$ is a new candidate trading-rate trajectory;
- $Y^0$ is the price impact trajectory corresponding to $u^0$;
- ICON predicts $\widehat{Y}^{\,0}$.

In the paper's experiments, $M=5$ context examples are often used. These context examples allow ICON to infer the current price impact operator. They are analogous to examples provided to a model in few-shot learning.

This setup is different from ordinary supervised learning. For a single pair $(u,Y)$, $u$ is the input and $Y$ is the output. However, in an ICON task, the already observed pairs $(u^m,Y^m)$ become part of the input context. The target is only the unobserved $Y^0$ corresponding to the query path $u^0$.

In the idealized version used in the paper's main numerical experiments, the context examples are pairs $(u,Y)$, where the price impact $Y$ is known. The paper also discusses the more realistic case where only execution-price pairs $(u,P)$ are available. In real markets, $Y$ is generally not directly observable because

$$
P_t
=
S_t-Y_t ,
$$

and the unaffected price $S_t$ is not observed. Therefore, using $(u,P)$ instead of $(u,Y)$ is more realistic but also more difficult, because the model must learn from noisy execution prices.

## 5. OCnet: Neural Network Solver for the Optimal Execution Policy

After ICON has inferred a surrogate price impact operator $\widehat{I}_\theta$ from the context examples, the paper uses this operator inside a downstream optimal execution solver called OCnet.

OCnet is a neural network policy of the form

$$
u_{t_i}
=
NN_{\vartheta}(t_i,\alpha_{t_i}),
$$

where $\vartheta$ denotes the neural network parameters. In the paper's deterministic numerical experiments, $\alpha_t\equiv 0$, so the policy effectively becomes

$$
u_{t_i}
=
NN_{\vartheta}(t_i).
$$

It is important to distinguish the roles of ICON and OCnet:

- ICON predicts price impact:

$$
u
\longmapsto
\widehat{Y}.
$$

- OCnet proposes a trading strategy:

$$
t
\longmapsto
u_t .
$$

During OCnet training, the ICON parameters are frozen. OCnet generates a candidate trading path $u$, ICON predicts the corresponding impact path

$$
\widehat{Y}
=
\widehat{I}_\theta(u),
$$

and the discretized execution objective is used to update the OCnet parameters $\vartheta$.

A simplified training loop is:

$$
\vartheta
\longrightarrow
u=NN_{\vartheta}(t),
$$

$$
u
\longrightarrow
\widehat{Y}
=
\widehat{I}_\theta(u),
$$

$$
(u,\widehat{Y},X)
\longrightarrow
J(u),
$$

$$
J(u)
\longrightarrow
\text{update }\vartheta .
$$

Thus, OCnet is not predicting price impact. It is learning the optimal trading speed path, while ICON acts as the differentiable surrogate model for price impact.

In the paper's original framework, ICON can be pretrained offline, but OCnet is optimized downstream after the current price impact operator has been inferred. This distinction is important for practical applications and motivates one of the future directions discussed later.

## 6. Numerical Testbed: Linear Propagator Models

The paper evaluates ICON-OCnet on linear propagator models because these models provide a tractable benchmark with known optimal strategies. In this class, price impact is modeled as

$$
Y_t
=
\bigl(I_\theta(u)\bigr)_t
=
\int_0^t
G(t-s)\lambda u_s\,ds .
$$

Here:

- $G$ is the propagator kernel;
- $\lambda$ is the push factor, also related to Kyle's lambda;
- $u_s$ is the past trading rate;
- $Y_t$ is the accumulated transient price impact.

The kernel $G(t-s)$ determines how much a past trade at time $s$ still affects the price at time $t$.

The paper studies several kernels.

### Exponential Kernel

$$
G(t)
=
e^{-\beta t}.
$$

Here, $\beta$ controls the speed of impact decay. A larger $\beta$ means faster decay. In this case, the impact process is Markovian and can be represented by an ordinary differential equation.

### Non-Singular Power-Law Kernel

$$
G(t)
=
\frac{1}{(\ell+t)^\gamma},
\qquad
\ell>0 .
$$

This kernel captures slower, multi-scale decay and produces non-Markovian dynamics.

### Singular Power-Law Kernel

$$
G(t)
=
t^{-\gamma},
\qquad
\gamma\in(0,0.5).
$$

This is a more difficult case because the kernel is singular near zero and also produces non-Markovian dynamics.

The paper uses these models as a proof-of-concept testbed. The broader method is not limited to linear propagator models; they are used because they make it possible to compare against ground truth optimal execution strategies.

## 7. Small-Scale Replication

To better understand the ICON training mechanism, I implemented a small-scale replication of the ICON operator learning step.

The replication does not reproduce the full Transformer-based ICON architecture. Instead, it uses a simplified MLP-based baseline to verify the core in-context operator learning pipeline:

$$
\left[
  (u^1,Y^1),
  \ldots,
  (u^5,Y^5),
  u^0
\right]
\longmapsto
Y^0 .
$$

### 7.1 Data Generation

The replication focuses only on the exponential propagator kernel:

$$
G(t)
=
e^{-\beta t}.
$$

The discrete price impact is computed by

$$
Y_{t_i}
\approx
\sum_{j=0}^{i}
e^{-\beta(t_i-t_j)}
\lambda u_{t_j}\Delta t .
$$

The experimental setup is:

- $T=1$;
- $N=100$;
- $\Delta t=0.01$;
- $\lambda\sim U([0.1,0.5])$;
- $\beta\sim U([0.462,9.011])$;
- 1,000 training regimes;
- 10 selling-rate trajectories per regime;
- 5 context examples per task;
- batch size 4;
- 10,000 training iterations.

For each sampled regime $\theta_i=(\lambda_i,\beta_i)$, I generated 10 smooth positive selling-rate trajectories $u$ and computed their corresponding impact paths $Y$ using the exponential linear propagator formula.

### 7.2 Model

For $M=5$ context examples and $N=100$ time steps, the model input is the flattened vector

$$
\left[
u^1,Y^1,
u^2,Y^2,
\ldots,
u^5,Y^5,
u^0
\right].
$$

Its dimension is

$$
5\times(100+100)+100
=
1100 .
$$

The target output is

$$
Y^0\in\mathbb{R}^{100}.
$$

The MLP architecture is:

```text
Input: 1100
Linear: 1100 -> 512
GELU
Linear: 512 -> 512
GELU
Linear: 512 -> 256
GELU
Linear: 256 -> 100
```

The model is trained using mean squared error between the predicted price impact path $\widehat{Y}^{\,0}$ and the true path $Y^0$.

### 7.3 Evaluation

The model is evaluated on unseen test regimes sampled from the same parameter ranges. For each test task, five context pairs are given to the model, and the model predicts the price impact path for one new query trajectory $u^0$.

The evaluation metric is relative $L^2$ error:

$$
\frac{
  \left\|\widehat{Y}-Y\right\|_2
}{
  \left\|Y\right\|_2
}.
$$

In local runs, the simplified MLP-ICON model achieved approximately:

```text
Mean relative L2 error: 0.06
Maximum relative L2 error: 0.20
```

This suggests that the simplified model successfully learns the core in-context operator learning task in the reduced exponential-kernel setting. It can infer the price impact relationship from five context examples and generalize to unseen regimes.

The result should not be interpreted as a full reproduction of the original paper, since the original ICON model uses a Transformer architecture, attention masking, multiple kernel classes, and larger-scale training. However, this replication verifies the central data-generation and in-context prediction logic.

## 8. Interpretation of the Paper's Contribution

The main contribution of the paper is to combine operator learning and optimal execution in a way that avoids explicit price impact parameter estimation.

Traditional approaches require choosing a specific price impact model and then estimating parameters. ICON-OCnet instead treats the impact model as an unknown operator and uses few-shot context examples to infer it. This is particularly attractive in financial markets, where price impact regimes are non-stationary and may vary across assets, time periods, liquidity conditions, and market stress regimes.

The paper's methodology has three important strengths.

First, ICON is data-driven. It can infer the impact operator from examples rather than relying only on explicit parametric estimation.

Second, ICON is few-shot adaptive. Once pretrained, it can adapt to a new environment through a small number of context examples.

Third, the ICON surrogate can be integrated into a downstream optimal control problem through OCnet. This connects operator learning directly to decision-making.

At the same time, the method also raises several practical questions. In real execution, $Y$ is not directly observed. The trader usually observes execution price $P$, but not the unaffected price $S$ or the isolated impact $Y$. Moreover, constructing reliable context examples may be difficult when the trader has limited recent own-execution history. Finally, OCnet may need to be optimized online after each new impact regime is inferred, which could be computationally expensive in real-time trading.

These limitations motivate several natural future directions.

## 9. Limitations and Future Directions

### 9.1 Reducing the Online Optimization Cost of OCnet

Although ICON can be pretrained offline, OCnet is optimized downstream after the current price impact operator has been inferred from context examples. In the paper's formulation, OCnet is trained for the surrogate operator $\widehat{I}_\theta$ obtained from the current context. If the OCnet parameters are randomly initialized for each new impact regime, the policy optimization may require many gradient steps.

This could be costly in real-time execution settings, especially if the strategy needs to be updated repeatedly during the trading day. A natural extension is therefore to pretrain a warm-start initialization for OCnet. Instead of starting from a random parameter vector,

$$
\vartheta_0,
$$

the downstream optimization would start from a pretrained initialization,

$$
\vartheta_{\mathrm{warm}}.
$$

The goal is not necessarily to train a universal OCnet that is immediately optimal for every environment. Rather, the goal is to learn an initialization that can adapt quickly to a newly inferred ICON surrogate operator.

This preserves the original two-step structure of the paper:

$$
\text{ICON infers } \widehat{I}_\theta,
$$

$$
\text{OCnet optimizes } u_t=NN_{\vartheta}(t,\alpha_t).
$$

The only change is that OCnet starts from a more informative initial parameter vector. This may reduce the number of gradient steps required for online optimization.

A more general version of this idea is meta-learning. One could train an initialization $\vartheta_{\mathrm{meta}}$ such that, after a small number of gradient updates under the current ICON surrogate operator, the policy becomes close to optimal for the current impact regime.

### 9.2 Market-State-Based Initialization of OCnet

A related question is how to choose a good initial value for the OCnet parameters. In the paper's numerical experiments, OCnet can be trained after ICON has inferred the current price impact environment. However, in real trading, the speed of this optimization matters. A better initialization may be especially useful when the execution algorithm needs to adapt quickly to changing market conditions.

One possible direction is to use directly observable market-state information to help initialize OCnet. For example, recent market data may include limit order book depth, bid-ask spread, order imbalance, signed order flow, realized volatility, intraday volume profile, and short-term price-volume trajectories. Visual representations such as candlestick charts, intraday price-volume charts, or limit order book heatmaps may also contain information about liquidity and order-flow pressure.

Rather than using these variables to explicitly estimate propagator parameters such as $\lambda$, $\beta$, or $\gamma$, they could be used to learn a better initialization for the OCnet policy network:

$$
z_{[t-L,t]}
\longrightarrow
\vartheta_{\mathrm{warm}},
$$

where $z_{[t-L,t]}$ denotes market information over a recent lookback window. The resulting initialization would then be refined through the original OCnet optimization objective using the ICON surrogate operator.

This approach keeps the original role of OCnet unchanged. OCnet still proposes candidate execution paths, ICON still predicts their price impact, and the execution objective still updates the OCnet parameters. The difference is that the optimization starts from a market-informed initialization rather than a random one.

This direction is connected to the broader question of whether market-state representations extracted from order book data, price-volume trajectories, or candlestick charts can improve adaptive optimal execution. In particular, such representations may help identify whether the current environment is liquid or illiquid, stable or stressed, and whether price impact is likely to be strong or weak. These features may be useful not only for price impact prediction, but also for initializing the downstream optimal execution policy.

---

## 10. Conclusion

The ICON-OCnet paper proposes an important step toward model-agnostic, data-driven optimal execution. By learning a price impact operator from in-context execution examples, ICON avoids explicit estimation of parametric propagator parameters. By using the inferred operator inside OCnet, the paper connects operator learning with downstream optimal control.

My small-scale replication verifies the core ICON learning mechanism in a reduced exponential-kernel setting. A simplified MLP-based model trained on synthetic propagator data achieves a mean relative $L^2$ error of approximately 0.06 on unseen test regimes, suggesting that the in-context operator learning pipeline is effective even in a small local experiment.

The paper also opens several practical research questions. First, OCnet may require expensive online optimization after each new impact regime is inferred; warm-start or meta-learned initialization may reduce this cost. Second, market-state information such as limit order book depth, price-volume trajectories, and candlestick charts may help construct better OCnet initializations or broader market-state representations for adaptive execution.

These directions suggest a broader research agenda: combining market-state representation learning, price impact operator learning, and optimal execution control in a unified framework.
